Bias and Variance

Concept
High Bias Underfitting: The model fails to capture the underlying patterns in the training data, resulting in poor performance even on examples it has already seen.
High Variance Overfitting: The model fits the training data extremely well but fails to generalise, capturing noise and dataset-specific quirks rather than true structure.
Data Splitting

To evaluate a model's bias and variance, the dataset is divided into two distinct subsets before any training takes place:

  • A training set, used to fit the model's parameters
  • A test set, held back entirely and used only to evaluate generalisation performance on unseen data
The absolute size of the test set matters more than its proportion of the total data. A test set of 10,000 examples gives roughly the same statistical reliability whether the full dataset contains 50,000 or 50 million examples. This is why, for datasets on the order of thousands to hundreds of thousands of examples, a 70/30 or 80/20 split is common. For datasets on the order of millions or more, proportions such as 99/1 or 98/2 are typical.

After training, performance is measured by the error on each subset. This error is a performance metric that may or may not be the same as the loss function used during training.

  • For regression, the error may be MSE (the same as the training loss), RMSE (for higher interpretability than MSE), MAE (Mean Absolute Error), or R² (coefficient of determination)
  • For binary classification, if the dataset is balanced, the error may be classification error rate (the proportion of misclassified examples), and if the dataset is imbalanced, it may be precision, recall, F1 score, etc.

Because the model has already been exposed to the training data, it will almost always achieve a lower error there than on fresh, unseen examples. Therefore, the test error \(J_{test}\) provides a more meaningful estimate of the model's true generalisation performance. For example, an overfitted model will exhibit a very low \(J_{training}\) alongside relatively high \(J_{test}\).

Model Selection

Bias/variance diagnosis lends itself to a natural strategy for choosing between different candidate models (that differ in regularisation strength, polynomial degree, network architecture, etc.). Simply train each model and pick the one that achieves the lowest \(J_{test}\). The problem is that this figure then becomes an optimistic estimate of true generalisation performance, because by selecting the model that happened to do best on this particular test set, we have implicitly optimised for it, introducing a subtle selection bias.

The standard remedy is to introduce a third subset, giving a three-way split of the full dataset:

  • A training set, used to fit the model's parameters
  • A validation set, used to compare and select between candidate models
  • A test set, used once, at the very end, to report an unbiased estimate of the chosen model's generalisation error
For datasets on the order of thousands to hundreds of thousands of examples, a 60/20/20 split is common. For datasets on the order of millions or more, proportions such as 98/1/1 or 99/0.5/0.5 are typical, since even a small fraction of a large dataset yields a statistically reliable evaluation set.

The workflow is then: compute the validation error \(J_{val}\) of each candidate model, select the one with the lowest value, and report its test error \(J_{test}\) as the final, unbiased estimate of generalisation performance.

Evaluating Error

But what do "high" and "low" mean? To ground these terms in context, we need a baseline — typically human-level performance on the same task, a competing algorithm's published result, or an established benchmark. From there, two key quantities can be defined:

  • Avoidable bias: \(J_{training} - J_{baseline}\)
    Measures how much worse the model is than the best achievable performance
  • Variance: \(J_{val} - J_{training}\)
    Measures how much performance degrades when moving from seen to unseen data

Comparing these two quantities gives a clear diagnostic picture:

  • If avoidable bias is higher than variance, the model is underfitting. The solution is to increase model capacity (e.g., by adding more features, using a more complex architecture, or reducing regularisation).
  • If variance is higher than avoidable bias, the model is overfitting. The solution is to reduce model capacity (e.g., by removing features, using a simpler architecture, or increasing regularisation).
Trade-off

Bias is the error from wrong assumptions. Variance is the error from sensitivity to the specific training set.

In classical machine learning, there was long believed to be an inherent trade-off between bias and variance. However, this traditional “bias–variance trade-off” perspective has become less dominant with the rise of deep learning. Modern neural networks are typically large and thus exhibit low bias, as they have sufficient capacity to capture complex patterns in training data. Surprisingly, even when these models are heavily overparameterised and achieve near-zero training error, they often still generalise well.

This counterintuitive behaviour is known as "double descent". It highlights that the relationship between bias and variance is more intricate than previously assumed, showing that increasing model capacity can, in some cases, improve generalisation, even beyond the point where training error has already been minimised.

Error Analysis

Concept

Complementing the evaluation of bias and variance, error analysis is an additional diagnostic approach. Rather than summarising performance as a single scalar, it involves manually inspecting misclassified or high-error examples and categorising them by shared characteristics.

For example, suppose a spam email classifier misclassifies one fifth of the 5,000 examples in the validation set. Reviewing all (or a subset of) these misclassified examples and grouping them by common traits reveals which error types are most prevalent. The same logic applies to regression: a house price model that produces large residuals for one fifth of its validation examples can be similarly dissected to surface recurring patterns.

Such an analysis helps identify where to focus improvement efforts (whether by collecting more targeted data, engineering better features, or correcting labelling mistakes) and it highlights which error types are rare enough to deprioritise.

Label Errors

One shared characteristic that may emerge from error analysis is incorrectly labelled examples in the validation set. In this case, the observed validation error is a mixture of errors caused by wrong labels and errors caused by genuine model failures. If the former is significant relative to the latter, correcting these labels becomes worthwhile.

Since the validation and test sets must always have the same distribution, any label corrections applied to the validation set must also be applied to the test set.
Limitations

While diagnosing bias and variance remains the primary step, error analysis provides practical, targeted insights for model refinement. However, it may be less effective in scenarios where even human judgement struggles. For instance, when spam messages are highly sophisticated and difficult to distinguish from legitimate emails, or when house prices are highly volatile and large residuals reflect genuine market noise rather than any correctable flaw in the data or model.
